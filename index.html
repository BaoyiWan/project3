Precipitation and Cropland Patterns Across the United States

This interactive visualization explores how precipitation patterns overlap with cropland density across the United States using NASA GIBS image layers. The values shown here are visual proxies rather than exact physical measurements, and the precipitation layer represents a selected daily snapshot rather than a long-term climate average.

Main Finding

The visualization shows intense cropland areas have a higher average precipitation proxy than sparse cropland and non-agricultural areas. However, the violin plot does not show much difference of distribution of overall precipitation, rain or snow proxy for Non-Agricultural, Sparse Crops, Intense Cropland in main data except for distribution of outliers. (Although for the overall precipitation and snow proxy, there are more data clustered at the top y axis and clustered at both top and bottom y axis in rain proxy for intense cropland.) So precipitation alone does not explain cropland density.

Write-up

Research Question

Our project asks: What is the spatial relationship between precipitation intensity and cropland density across the United States? Instead of trying to prove that precipitation causes more crops to grow, we focus on the spatial relationship between cropland distribution and precipitation intensity.

Data and Transformation

We used NASA GIBS image layers for cropland distribution and precipitation rate. Since these layers are image-based, we extracted color intensity values from the pixels and used them as visual proxies. The green channel from the cropland layer was used as an approximate cropland-density proxy, while the RGB intensity from the precipitation layer was used as an approximate precipitation proxy. We then sampled the pixel-level data to keep the D3 visualization fast in the browser.

Visual Encoding

The violin plot uses the y-axis to represent overall precipitation, rain or snow proxy for Non-Agricultural, Sparse Crops and Intense Cropland. Color represents the crop-zone category: non-agricultural, sparse crops, or intense cropland. The bar chart summarizes the average selected precipitation proxy for each crop-zone group, making the group-level pattern easier to compare. An interactive US map visualization displays the geographic distribution of sampled data points, with a yellow-to-red color scale encoding precipitation intensity values. This spatial view complements the statistical summaries and provides geographic context for the precipitation and cropland patterns.

Interaction Design

The precipitation dropdown lets users switch between overall precipitation, rain proxy, and snow proxy. The crop-zone dropdown lets users filter the view to focus on one land-use category at a time. Hover tooltips reveal the exact proxy values and approximate geographic location for each sampled point. All three visualizations—the violin plot, bar chart, and interactive map—update dynamically and synchronously when dropdown selections change, providing coordinated views of the same underlying data from different analytical perspectives.

Development Process

Our group first explored the data in Python by creating several static graphs, including an overlay map, violin plot, and grouped bar charts. After choosing the final research question, we exported cleaned and sampled data into CSV files and rebuilt the visualization in D3. The most time-consuming part was transforming image-based map layers into a format that could be used for an interactive browser visualization. Based on feedback, we added an interactive US map with D3 to better show the spatial relationships between precipitation and cropland patterns across geographic regions.

For the final implementation, part of the group focused on Python data extraction and exploratory graphs, while another part focused on converting the cleaned data into CSV files and rebuilding the visualization in D3. In total, we spent about 8 people-hours on data cleaning, 6 people-hours on exploratory analysis, and 8 people-hours on the D3 webpage and interaction design.

Limitations

The values in this visualization should be interpreted as approximate visual intensity proxies, not exact physical measurements. The precipitation layer also represents a selected snapshot rather than a long-term climate average. Because of this, our conclusion focuses on visual overlap and association rather than causal impact.
